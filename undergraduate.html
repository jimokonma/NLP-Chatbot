Philosophy

The development and running of our programme are based on the philosophy that rapid integration between academic studies and real-life applications in both the industry and public service is what makes a good Engineering programme. It is following this philosophy that, in all our core courses, students are encouraged to collect data and test engineering models in real life situations. For the fact that we are concerned with man-machine work systems, this has been very successfully accomplished.

Objectives

The objectives of the B.Sc. Industrial and Production Engineering Programme are:

  1. To educate and train students to have broad engineering background to enable them work successfully as Industrial and Production Engineers in Industries and Public Service.
  2. To graduate Industrial and Production Engineers with sufficient computing, communication and engineering management skills with the intent to enhance their professional growth, career leadership and global competitiveness.
  3. To provide Industrial and Production Engineering graduates with sufficient knowledge and challenges of the discipline which will enable them enter into and successfully complete postgraduate studies.

Undergraduate Programme

Candidates are admitted into the BSc. Degree programmes of the Faculty in any of the following three ways: - through University Matriculation Examination (UTME), by Direct Entry, or through Inter-University Transfer.

  1. University Matriculation Examinations (UME) Entry Mode:
    In addition to an acceptable pass in UME, candidates must have:
    1. Credit passes in Senior Secondary School Certificate (SSCE) English Language, Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry and any other subject.
    2. WASCE/G.C.E. (O' Level) credit passes in English Language, Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry and any other subject.
    3. Satisfactory performance at an Interactive Post-JAMB session.
  2. Direct Entry Mode:
    1. Candidates with GCE (Advanced Level) in at least Mathematics and Physics plus 1(a) or 1(b) above may be admitted direct (without examination) into 200 level.
      1. Candidates with OND or equivalent qualification may be admitted direct(without examination) into 200 level
      2. Candidates with HND or equivalent qualification may be admitted direct(without examination) into 300 1evel.
      3. In both cases, candidates must have obtained an upper credit and an average of at least 65% in their final examinations plus 1(a) or 1(b) above.
  3. Inter-University Transfer Mode:
    1. Students can transfer into 200 level courses provided they have the relevant qualifications.
    2. No candidate shall be admitted from other Universities unless the Faculty and the Department are satisfied that the grades obtained by such candidates for courses in which they have had instructions can be properly assigned relevant units.

COURSE REQUIREMENT AND STRUCTURE

The students are introduced to the requisite mathematics, physics and social sciences in the first two years. At the same time, the students are exposed to the concepts necessary to comprehend, appreciate, design and analyse work systems and production processes later in the programme. Thus, they take courses such as Workshop practice, Engineering Drawing, Mechanics and Computer Programming.

In the third year, students take general courses like applied Physics and Social Sciences courses as Engineering Economics, Engineering Statistics, Operation Research, Materials Science, Manufacturing Science, Work Study, Project Planning and Control, Machine tools, etc. They also take courses like Strength of Materials, Applied Thermodynamics, Design of Machine elements and Mechanics of Machines, etc. which introduce them to the design processes.

In the last two years, students take courses in which previously learned principles are applied to design, analyse, operate and control effective and efficient work systems. These are done either as laboratory/workshop exercises or in real-life situations. Such subjects include Process Design, Facilities and Industrial Systems Design, Maintenance Engineering and Human Factors Engineering. Others include Management Systems Design, Industrial Quality Control, Tools/Fixtures Design, Robotics, etc.

It is mandatory for a student to spend a total of twelve (12) months outside of formal studies for Industrial attachment. After the second and third years respectively, students proceed on three (3) months Industrial attachment during the long vacation. In the fourth year, the students are in the University for Formal Academic Work only during the first semester. During the 2nd semester, the students are attached to the Industry for Students Industrial Work Experience Scheme (SIWES) Training.

To be awarded the degree of Bachelor of Science in Industrial and Production Engineering, candidates must satisfy the regulations for the B.Sc. degree in the Faculty of Technology. Candidates must also satisfy all departmental requirements. Candidates are expected to select their courses in consultation with their course advisers.

A student is expected to pass a minimum of 150 courses including all compulsory courses.

The core Industrial and Production Engineering courses can be classified into the following areas:

  1. Ergonomics and Human Factors Engineering:

    This deals with the design of man-machine work systems with emphasis on worker relation to his/her general work environment (physical facilities, fellow worker and ambient conditions). Some of the courses in this area are: Work Systems Design, Methods and Safety Engineering. Work Measurement and Human Factors Engineering.

  2. Engineering Management:

    This deals with the use of scientific techniques in measuring, evaluating, choosing alternative courses of actions, controlling and predicting the outcomes of operations. It covers such courses as Engineering Economics, Replacement Theory, Production Forecasting, Decision Theory, Engineering Organizations as well as Project Planning and Control.

  3. Operations Research:

    This combines mathematical modelling techniques with the principles of optimization to identify the most effective and efficient components of a work system. Mathematical Programming, Network Flow Analysis and Engineering Statistics, Queuing Theory are some of the courses in Operations Research.

  4. Production/Manufacturing:

    This deals with the principles and techniques of the design and analysis of effective, efficient, safe, reliable and maintainable production systems. Also, deals with product development. It includes such courses as Process Analysis, Synthesis and Design; Production and Inventory Systems Design and Control; Quality Control; Maintenance System Design; Plant Layout and Location, product development, manufacturing processes etc.

  5. Systems Analysis:

    This is the application of mathematical and computer modelling techniques to design and analyse complex systems. The areas covered include: information systems, simulation, computer communication techniques.

STUDENTS' INDUSTRIAL WORK EXPERIENCE

There are three periods students are sent out for industrial experience at the end of successful 200, 300 and 400 levels. Associated with these periods are TIT 299 (2 units), TIT 399 (3 units) and TIT 499 (6 units) course equivalents, respectively. While the first two are required the last one is compulsory. Students are therefore, expected to submit a report and a work diary at the end of each training for evaluation. The Industrial Coordination Centre of the University headed by highly qualified and vastly experienced director sources placements for students', supervises, conducts seminars and effectively manages the programme. The members of the academic staff also pay regular site visits to ensure that students are making the best use of the industrial training (IT). The student IT and seminars are graded by the academic staff.

ENTREPRENEURSHIP

The role of educational interventions in entrepreneurship development is now widely acknowledged in developing and sustaining a growing national or global economy. It has been proved that entrepreneurs could be created through teaching, training and counselling interventions as well. Entrepreneurship Development has thus become an important part of our system in the department. Generally, all our courses have entrepreneurial flavours which are emphasised during interactions with the students. Also, the TIE 526 (The Special Topics in Industrial Engineering) for the final year is used as platforms teach specific.

Entrepreneurial skills. Some of our Alumni who are successful entrepreneurs are brought in on regular basis to share their experiences with and motivate our students. The department has a working memorandum of understanding with PEDI, Leyland Automation Academy.

Recently, the university got a state-of-the-art automation centre donated by Nigeria Liquefied natural gas limited (NLNG). This gas added great value to the students' practical experience. Our students also take the introduced entrepreneurial course run by the University's Centre for Entrepreneurship as a required course.
